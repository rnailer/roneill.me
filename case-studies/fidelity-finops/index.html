01

Turning VM utilisation data into clear next steps

The AI/ML FinOps chatbot let engineering teams ask questions about their cloud estate, which machines were underused, where money was leaking, what looked anomalous. It worked, but only if you knew what to ask. I shaped the chatbot's front-end UX, then independently designed and built a VM Utilisation Dashboard, a visual layer inside the same product that surfaces what needs attention before anyone types a question.

01Overview

A conversational product with a visual layer alongside it

Behind the chatbot, a daily pipeline pulled utilisation metrics, CPU and network in/out, from virtual machines and databases across the cloud estate, and served them to a conversational interface.

The front-end work covered the usual chatbot questions: how many prompts to show, how to move people from guided prompts into freeform questions, how to gather context, how feedback should work. I owned that UX direction, then went a step further and designed a dashboard to sit alongside it.

02The problem

A chat-only product makes users do the hard part

The chatbot could answer detailed FinOps questions, but the value was locked behind knowing the right question. To find underused machines or the biggest savings, you already had to suspect they were there.

That's a lot to ask of a busy engineer who just wants to know whether their estate is healthy.

03Research

“Show me, don't make me ask”

I ran the chatbot through moderated usability sessions, affinity-mapped the results, and prioritised with MoSCoW. One theme surfaced again and again: people wanted to see the data, not interrogate it. “Would love a graph to visualise this.” “Add labels and structure so I can scan it.” “Just show me what needs attention.”

Answers that were accurate but dense made users work to interpret them. The research pointed clearly past a better chatbot, toward a visual layer.

The signal

It wasn't “make the chatbot smarter.” It was “let me scan before I ask.”

04The design

A dashboard that answers one question first

I designed the dashboard around a single question: what needs attention, and what should I do next? You enter an Application, Product, or Product Line ID; the dashboard resolves the right level of data and shows a colour-coded summary.

For an application, every VM appears as a card grouped by environment, DEV, QA, PROD, showing its name, average CPU, a traffic-light status, and a review flag where relevant. The traffic-light model does the interpreting so the user doesn't have to:

  • Green, correctly utilised
  • Orange, underutilised
  • Red, severely underutilised
  • Grey, not enough data to score yet
  • Flagged, Max CPU spiked, so it may need a closer look rather than simple downsizing

A simple score, the ratio of green machines, rolls the same logic up the hierarchy: Product Line → Product → Application → VM. Whatever level you enter at, status reads the same way.

05Insight → action

Connecting insight to action

The dashboard wasn't built to report; it was built to move people to the next step. For an underutilised machine, the design put the actions right there: right-sizing guidance, a button to open the chatbot pre-scoped to that VM, and a button to raise a resizing ticket.

That join is the point, scan visually first, drop into chat for the follow-up, escalate without leaving the page.

The shift

From “here is the data” to “here is what needs attention, and here is what you can do about it.”

06A decision

A decision from the research

After the usability round, VM cards gained Max CPU alongside Average CPU. Average alone could make a machine look idle when it was actually spiking periodically; Max CPU let reviewers tell “genuinely underused” from “quiet most of the time, critical occasionally.”

A small addition that changed how trustworthy the call felt.

07Outcome

From question-led to discovery-led

The work gave the team a clear, reviewable direction for extending the chatbot into a visual, self-service experience, and moved the product from question-led to discovery-led.

Instead of asking users to start with the perfect prompt, it surfaces the issues first and lets the chatbot handle the depth. I built the prototype independently from the brief; it's the interactive piece at the top of this page.
